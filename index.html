About Him

John J. Hopfield The Nobel Prize in Physics 2024 Born: 15 July 1933, Chicago, IL, USA Affiliation at the time of the award: Princeton University, Princeton, NJ, USA Prize motivation: “for foundational discoveries and inventions that enable machine learning with artificial neural networks”

At Work, When we talk about artificial intelligence, we often mean machine learning using artificial neural networks. This technology was originally inspired by the structure of the brain. In an artificial neural network, the brain’s neurons are represented by nodes that have different values. In 1982, John Hopfield invented a network that uses a method for saving and recreating patterns. He found inspiration in physics' models of how many small parts in a system affect the system as a whole. The invention became important in, for example, image analysis.

Early life and education

John J. Hopfield

John Joseph Hopfield was born on July 15, 1933, in Chicago, Illinois, to physicists John Joseph Hopfield (originally Jan Józef Chmielewski) and Helen Hopfield (née Staff). WIKIPEDIA Growing up in an environment steeped in scientific inquiry, he developed an early fascination with understanding how things work. His parents encouraged this curiosity, allowing him to dismantle household items to explore their inner workings, provided he could reassemble them. FISHER COLLEGE This hands-on exploration fostered a deep appreciation for physics, which he perceived not merely as a subject but as a lens through which to comprehend the physical world. CORNELL NEWS Hopfield pursued his passion academically, earning a Bachelor of Arts in physics from Swarthmore College in 1954, followed by a Ph.D. in physics from Cornell University in 1958. WIKIPEDIA His doctoral dissertation, titled "A quantum-mechanical theory of the contribution of excitons to the complex dielectric constant of crystals," was supervised by Albert Overhauser. WIKIPEDIA This solid foundation in physics set the stage for his pioneering contributions across multiple scientific disciplines.

Career

John J. Hopfield

He spent two years in the theory group at Bell Laboratories working on optical properties of semiconductors with David Gilbert Thomas and later on a quantitative model to describe the cooperative behavior of hemoglobin in collaboration with Robert G. Shulman. Subsequently, he became a faculty member at the University of California, Berkeley (physics, 1961-1964), Princeton University (physics, 1964-1980), California Institute of Technology (Caltech, chemistry and biology, 1980-1997), and again at Princeton (1997), where he is the Howard A. Prior Professor of Molecular Biology, emeritus.
In 1976, he participated in a science short film on the structure of hemoglobin, featuring Linus Pauling.
His former PhD students include Gerald Mahan (PhD in 1964), Bertrand Halperin (1965), Steven Girvin (1977), Terry Sejnowski (1978), Erik Winfree (1998), José Onuchic (1987), Li Zhaoping (1990), and David J. C. MacKay (1992).

Views on artificial intelligence

John J. Hopfield In March 2023, Hopfield signed an open letter titled "Pause Giant AI Experiments", calling for a pause on the training of artificial intelligence systems more powerful than GPT-4. The letter, signed by over 30,000 individuals including AI researchers Yoshua Bengio and Stuart Russell, cited risks such as human obsolescence and society-wide loss of control. Upon being jointly awarded the 2024 Nobel Prize in Physics, Hopfield revealed he was very unnerved by recent advances in AI capabilities, and said "as a physicist, I'm very unnerved by something which has no control". In a followup press conference in Princeton University, Hopfield compared AI with discovery of nuclear fission, which led to nuclear weapons and nuclear power.

Awards and honors

John J. Hopfield Hopfield received a Sloan Research Fellowship in 1962 and, like his father, he received a Guggenheim Fellowship in 1968.
Hopfield was elected as a member of the American Physical Society in 1969, a member of the National Academy of Sciences in 1973, a member of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences in 1975, and a member of the American Philosophical Society in 1988.
He was the President of the APS in 2006.
In 1969, Hopfield and David Gilbert Thomas were awarded the Oliver E. Buckley Prize of condensed matter physics by the APS "for their joint work combining theory and experiment which has advanced the understanding of the interaction of light with solids."
In 1983, he was awarded the MacArthur Foundational Prize by the MacArthur Fellows Program.
In 1985, Hopfield received the Golden Plate Award of the American Academy of Achievement and the Max Delbruck Prize in Biophysics by the APS.
In 1988, he received the Michelson–Morley Award by Case Western Reserve University.
Hopfield received the Neural Networks Pioneer Award in 1997 by the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers.
He was awarded the Dirac Medal of the International Centre for Theoretical Physics in 2001 "for important contributions in an impressively broad spectrum of scientific subjects," including "an entirely different organizing principle in olfaction" and "a new principle in which neural function can take advantage of the temporal structure of the 'spiking' interneural communication."
Hopfield received the Harold Pender Award in 2002 for his accomplishments in computational neuroscience and neural engineering from the Moore School of Electrical Engineering, University of Pennsylvania.
He received the Albert Einstein World Award of Science in 2005 in the field of life sciences.
In 2007, he gave the Fritz London Memorial Lecture at Duke University, titled "How Do We Think So Fast? From Neurons to Brain Computation."
Hopfield received the IEEE Frank Rosenblatt Award in 2009 for his contributions in understanding information processing in biological systems.
In 2012, he was awarded the Swartz Prize by the Society for Neuroscience.
In 2019, he was awarded the Benjamin Franklin Medal in Physics by the Franklin Institute, and in 2022 he shared the Boltzmann Medal award in statistical physics with Deepak Dhar.
He was jointly awarded the 2024 Nobel Prize in Physics with Geoffrey E. Hinton for "foundational discoveries and inventions that enable machine learning with artificial neural networks."

Conclusion

The references highlight the groundbreaking work of John J. Hopfield, whose contributions span various fields such as physics, neuroscience, and artificial intelligence. His work on neural networks, especially the Hopfield network, has had profound implications for computational models of the brain and optimization problems. Through his research, Hopfield has influenced both theoretical physics and practical AI applications. The ongoing recognition of his achievements, including the Nobel Prize in Physics 2024, underscores the lasting impact of his pioneering work on the scientific community.
